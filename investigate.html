Exhibit A — Homer, Iliad Book 3
The Duel That Should Have Ended Everything
Paris and Menelaus fight a single combat duel. The winner gets Helen. The loser's army goes home. What happens?

Menelaus wins. Decisively. He drags Paris by the helmet toward the Greek lines. The war should be over.

Aphrodite intervenes. She snatches Paris away in a cloud of mist and deposits him in Helen's bedroom. The Trojans violate the truce. The war continues — not because of Helen, but because the gods won't let the agreed resolution stand.

Key finding: The Greeks and Trojans agreed to settle it. The gods overruled them.

Exhibit B — Homer, Iliad Book 6
Hector Condemns His Own Brother
Hector confronts Paris inside the walls of Troy. What does Troy's greatest defender actually say to him?

Hector doesn't call Paris a thief. He doesn't accuse him of stealing a woman. He calls him a man who violated the sacred bond between host and guest.

In Greek: Paris broke xenia. He sat at Menelaus's table, ate his food, slept under his roof — and then took his wife. The crime isn't theft. It's the violation of a divine covenant.

Key finding: Even Troy's own hero identified xenia — not Helen — as the cause.

Exhibit C — Homer, Iliad Book 13
Zeus's Law of Xenia
Zeus is called "Zeus Xenios" — protector of guests and hosts. What does this title mean for the Trojan War?

Zeus Xenios enforces the covenant of xenia across the entire Greek world. When a host welcomes a guest — food, shelter, gifts — both are bound by divine law. Neither can harm the other.

Paris didn't just break a social norm. He violated a law enforced by the king of the gods himself. The Greek coalition that sailed to Troy wasn't an army fighting for a stolen woman. It was a divinely mandated enforcement action.

Key finding: The war was divine punishment, not personal revenge.

Exhibit D — Homer, Odyssey Book 24
The Pattern Repeats
The suitors in Odysseus's house commit the same crime as Paris. What happens to them?

The suitors eat Odysseus's food. Sleep under his roof. Court his wife. They violate xenia from the other direction — as guests who abuse the host's home.

Odysseus kills every single one of them. Homer frames this as justice — the same divine framework that condemned Paris. The entire Odyssey is a second xenia case study.

Key finding: Homer uses the same crime-and-punishment pattern across both epics. Xenia violation = annihilation.

Exhibit E — Cross-Cultural Parallel
The Universal Pattern
Other ancient cultures had the same concept. What does this tell us about xenia?

Hebrew tradition: Abraham's hospitality to the three strangers is a foundational story. Sodom's destruction is framed as punishment for violating guest-right — not the sins modern readers assume.

Norse culture: breaking hospitality bonds triggered blood feuds that lasted generations. The guest-host relationship was as sacred as kinship.

Hindu tradition: Atithi Devo Bhava — "the guest is god." Violating this brought cosmic consequences.

Key finding: Xenia wasn't uniquely Greek. It was a universal ancient law. Paris violated the most sacred rule of the Bronze Age world.

Section 03 — Case Verdict
Investigator's Conclusion

The Trojan War was not caused by a stolen wife.
It was caused by the violation of xenia —
a divine covenant enforced by Zeus himself.

Greece didn't mobilise for Helen.
Greece mobilised because Paris broke the oldest law in the ancient world.

Sources: Homer, Iliad Books 3, 6, 13, 24 · Odyssey Book 24 · 8th century BCE
