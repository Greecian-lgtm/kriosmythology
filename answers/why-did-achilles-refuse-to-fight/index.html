Why Did Achilles Refuse to Fight?

The popular answer is that Achilles sulked. His prize, Briseis, was taken by Agamemnon, and he withdrew to his tent in wounded pride. That is the retelling version. Homer's Iliad tells something more precise.

Homer opens the Iliad with one word: menis. Not ordinary anger. The word Homer chose is used for divine wrath — the kind that belongs to gods. No other mortal in the Iliad receives menis. Only Achilles and the gods. Homer is telling you from line one that this is not sulking. This is strategic withdrawal backed by divine endorsement.

Achilles goes to his mother, Thetis, immediately. He asks her to petition Zeus to let the Trojans win — to push the Greeks back until Agamemnon is forced to acknowledge that he cannot win without Achilles. This is not the behaviour of someone emotionally overwhelmed. This is a calculated sequence: withdraw, create conditions for your return to be publicly necessary, return on terms you dictate.

There is also the question of purpose. Achilles knows his fate. Thetis told him: a short glorious life, or a long obscure one. Achilles is at Troy for kleos — undying fame. Agamemnon's insult didn't just wound his pride. It publicly destroyed the public recognition that his death was meant to purchase. Without that recognition, the death is meaningless. Achilles isn't sulking. He is protecting the only thing that makes dying at Troy worth doing.

The primary source — Homer's Iliad, Book 1 — supports none of the sulking interpretation. Achilles acts with speed, precision, and divine backing. Every death in the poem flows from his withdrawal. Homer calls it menis in line one. That tells you everything.
