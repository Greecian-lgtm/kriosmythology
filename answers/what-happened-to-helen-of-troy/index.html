What Happened to Helen of Troy After the War?

The war ended. Troy burned. But what happened to the woman at the center of it? Ancient sources give dramatically different answers — including one version where Helen was never in Troy to begin with.

What happened to Helen after the Trojan War?

In Homer's Odyssey, Helen is back in Sparta with Menelaus, apparently reconciled and living in royal comfort. When Odysseus's son Telemachus visits in Book 4, she and Menelaus host him graciously. Homer offers no judgment — she is simply a queen again. Later sources are less forgiving: some say she was exiled to Rhodes after Menelaus died, where she was hanged by a Rhodian queen whose husband had died at Troy.

Did Menelaus take Helen back after the Trojan War?

According to most ancient sources, yes. The story goes that Menelaus intended to kill Helen when Troy fell, but when he saw her again — either her beauty or Aphrodite's power — he dropped his sword. Euripides dramatizes this moment in The Trojan Women and Helen. Stesichorus, the 6th-century BCE poet, went further: he claimed Helen never went to Troy at all, and that only her phantom traveled with Paris. Helen herself spent the war in Egypt with the righteous king Proteus.

Was Helen guilty for the Trojan War?

Ancient sources are divided. Homer's Iliad shows Helen with self-awareness and even regret — she calls herself a 'shameless bitch' (Book 6). But she also yields to Aphrodite's compulsion. Gorgias of Leontini wrote a famous rhetorical Encomium of Helen around 400 BCE arguing she was entirely blameless: either the gods compelled her, or Paris used force, or love's power is irresistible. The debate never settled. Euripides staged both versions — guilty Helen and innocent Helen — in different plays.

What does history say about Helen of Troy?

There is no historical evidence for an individual woman named Helen who caused the Trojan War. The city of Troy (Hisarlik, Turkey) is archaeologically real, and there is evidence of destruction layers in the Bronze Age. But the war's cause — if there was a single war — is unknown. Most scholars believe the Helen myth is a traditional story that accumulated around a historical memory of Bronze Age conflict between Mycenaean Greeks and Anatolian cities.
