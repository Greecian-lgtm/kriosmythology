Who Was Perseus?

Perseus is one of the oldest Greek heroes in the written record. What Hesiod and Pindar say about him is considerably stranger than the Hollywood version.

Who was Perseus in Greek mythology?

Perseus was the son of Zeus and Danaë, a mortal princess of Argos. His grandfather Acrisius had locked Danaë in a bronze chamber after an oracle warned that her son would kill him — Zeus reached her anyway as a shower of gold. Perseus grew up on the island of Seriphus and was sent on what was meant to be a suicide mission by King Polydectes: fetch the head of Medusa, one of the three Gorgon sisters, the only mortal one. He succeeded with divine assistance from Athena and Hermes.

How did Perseus kill Medusa?

The gods equipped Perseus for the task: Hermes gave him a sickle (or adamantine sword), Athena gave him a mirrored shield so he could look at Medusa indirectly without being turned to stone, and he acquired winged sandals, a kibisis (bag for the head), and the cap of Hades that made him invisible. Hesiod's Theogony and the Shield of Heracles both describe the encounter. Perseus looked at Medusa's reflection, cut off her head while she slept, put it in the bag, and escaped using the winged sandals while her immortal sisters pursued him.

What happened after Perseus killed Medusa?

Flying home with Medusa's head, Perseus saw Andromeda chained to a rock as a sacrifice to a sea monster — her mother Cassiopeia had boasted she was more beautiful than the sea nymphs (Nereids), offending Poseidon. Perseus killed the monster, married Andromeda, and continued home. He eventually used Medusa's head to turn the Titan Atlas to stone (creating the Atlas Mountains, per some sources) and turned his enemy Polydectes and his court to stone. He fulfilled the oracle accidentally: when competing in athletic games at Larissa, his discus throw went off course and killed Acrisius in the crowd.

Did Perseus found any cities?

Ancient sources credit Perseus with founding Mycenae, the great Bronze Age citadel in the Argolid. According to Pausanias's Description of Greece (2nd century CE), Perseus founded the city and named it either after his sword's cap (mykes) or after a spring mushroom he found there. This makes Perseus the mythological ancestor of the Mycenaean civilization — the Bronze Age culture that Homer's Iliad describes, and whose actual remains survive at Mycenae to this day.
