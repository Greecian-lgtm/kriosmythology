What Is the Trojan Horse?

Homer's Iliad — the great poem of the Trojan War — never describes the Trojan Horse. That detail is tucked into a later poem. Here's what the ancient sources actually say, and what the archaeology suggests.

What is the Trojan Horse story?

The Trojan Horse was a large wooden horse constructed by the Greek warrior Epeius on the advice of Odysseus. Greek soldiers hid inside it; the rest of the Greek fleet sailed away and pretended to leave. The Trojans debated what to do with it — some wanted to destroy it, others to bring it inside as an offering to Athena. The priest Laocoön warned against it ('I fear Greeks bearing gifts') and was killed by sea serpents. The Trojans dragged it inside. At night the Greeks emerged, opened the city gates for their returning army, and Troy fell.

Did Homer describe the Trojan Horse?

Strikingly, no. Homer's Iliad ends before the fall of Troy, and the Horse is not in it. The Odyssey mentions it only briefly: in Book 4, Helen refers to the Horse Odysseus devised; in Book 8, the bard Demodocus sings about it at the court of the Phaeacians. The detailed narrative comes from later sources: Virgil's Aeneid, the mythographer Apollodorus, and Quintus of Smyrna's Posthomerica. The oldest poem that told the full story, the Little Iliad, survives only in fragments.

Is the Trojan Horse historically real?

No physical evidence supports a literal wooden horse. Most scholars interpret it as a later addition to the Troy legend — possibly a metaphor for a siege engine, a ship figurehead, or a diplomatic ruse. Some scholars, including Michael Wood, have suggested 'the horse' was a code name for a battering ram, since such devices were sometimes given animal names in Near Eastern military texts. The city of Troy (Hisarlik, Turkey) shows real Bronze Age destruction layers, but the cause remains unknown.

Who built the Trojan Horse?

Ancient sources attribute its construction to Epeius (or Epeus), a craftsman and boxer among the Greeks. Apollodorus says Epeius built it on Athena's instructions. Virgil's Aeneid names him the builder. He is one of mythology's most interesting minor figures — a man famous not for fighting but for craft, who built the instrument that destroyed a civilization. The Odyssey's brief references suggest the scheme was Odysseus's idea, making it a collaboration between Odysseus's cunning and Epeius's technical skill.
