Why Did Athena Punish Medusa?

The short answer from the primary sources: Athena punished Medusa because Poseidon assaulted her in Athena's own temple. Under ancient Greek religious law, a sacred space defiled by what occurred within it directed punishment at the person present — not the aggressor. Athena punished Medusa. Poseidon faced no consequence.

This account comes from Ovid's Metamorphoses, written in the 1st century BCE. Ovid describes Medusa as having been beautiful before her transformation — a priestess of Athena, assaulted by Poseidon inside the goddess's temple. Athena's response was to transform Medusa's hair into snakes and make her gaze capable of turning men to stone.

The older sources — Hesiod's Theogony (c. 700 BCE) and Pindar's Pythian Ode 12 (498 BCE) — do not give the full account of the transformation. Hesiod identifies Medusa as the mortal Gorgon, distinguishing her from her immortal sisters. Pindar describes her as beautiful. Neither gives the temple assault. That detail enters the written record with Ovid, though oral tradition may have carried it earlier.

The logic of Athena's punishment is rooted in Greek religious pollution law. A temple made impure by a sexual act required purification. The priestess present — regardless of her role in what occurred — bore the ritual pollution. This is not a moral framework recognisable to modern readers, but it is consistent with how ancient Greek religion understood defilement: it attached to place and person by proximity, not by intention or guilt.

The result is a character who reads, across all the sources together, as a tragedy. Medusa was a priestess punished for surviving something done to her. The Gorgon face — the weapon her severed head becomes in Perseus's hands — is the face of someone transformed by the system that was supposed to protect her. This reading is not a modern invention. It is in the primary sources.
