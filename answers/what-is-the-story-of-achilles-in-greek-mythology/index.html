What Is the Story of Achilles in Greek Mythology?

By Stavros Krios · Primary sources: Homer Iliad, Pindar, Apollodorus

Direct Answer

Achilles is the greatest Greek warrior in Homer's Iliad. The poem opens with the word menis — rage — which is his defining characteristic. His withdrawal from battle after a quarrel with Agamemnon, and his return after the death of his companion Patroclus, drive the entire narrative. He is killed by an arrow from Paris, guided by Apollo — not by a heel wound. The heel vulnerability does not appear in Homer.

The story of Achilles' heel does not appear in Homer's Iliad. It is a later tradition, recorded in Roman-era sources. This is one of the most common misconceptions about what the primary source actually says.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Achilles heel story in Homer?

No. Homer's Iliad does not mention a heel vulnerability. Achilles is killed by an arrow from Paris, guided by Apollo — but no special weakness is described. The heel tradition comes from later Roman-era sources, not from the original Greek primary source.

What is the relationship between Achilles and Patroclus?

Patroclus is Achilles's closest companion in the Iliad. When Achilles refuses to fight, Patroclus borrows his armor and enters battle, is killed by Hector, and his death brings Achilles back into the war in a rage. Homer uses the word hetairos — companion — for their relationship.

What primary source covers Achilles?

Homer's Iliad, 8th century BC, is the foundational text. Pindar's Isthmian and Nemean Odes reference Achilles. The later Achilleid by Statius (Roman era) covers his early life and introduces the heel tradition.
