What Are the 12 Labours of Hercules?

By Stavros Krios · Primary sources: Apollodorus Library, Pindar, Diodorus Siculus

Direct Answer

The 12 Labours of Hercules were tasks imposed by King Eurystheus of Tiryns as penance after Hercules killed his wife and children in a fit of madness sent by Hera. The canonical twelve: Nemean Lion, Lernaean Hydra, Ceryneian Hind, Erymanthian Boar, Augean Stables, Stymphalian Birds, Cretan Bull, Mares of Diomedes, Belt of Hippolyta, Cattle of Geryon, Apples of the Hesperides, Cerberus from the Underworld.

The name Hercules is Roman. In all Greek primary sources he is Heracles — meaning "glory of Hera," which is darkly ironic given that Hera is his greatest enemy. The canonical list of twelve was standardized by Apollodorus in his Library, written in the 2nd century BC.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why did Hercules have to do the 12 labours?

He killed his wife and children in a fit of divinely-sent madness. The Oracle at Delphi told him to serve King Eurystheus of Tiryns for twelve years as penance. This is the version in Apollodorus's Library — the most complete ancient account.

What primary source lists all 12 Labours?

Apollodorus's Library, written in the 2nd century BC, gives the most complete canonical list. Individual labours appear in Pindar, Euripides, and Diodorus Siculus, but Apollodorus standardized the full sequence of twelve.

Is Hercules Greek or Roman?

Hercules is the Roman name. The original Greek name is Heracles — meaning "glory of Hera," which is darkly ironic since Hera is his chief tormentor throughout his life. In all Greek primary sources he is always Heracles.
