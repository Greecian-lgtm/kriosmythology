Who Was Ariadne?

She saved Theseus from the Minotaur's maze. He left her on a beach. What the ancient sources say happened next is stranger than any version you've heard.

Who was Ariadne in Greek mythology?

Ariadne was the daughter of King Minos of Crete and the granddaughter of the god Zeus. When Theseus arrived in Crete as part of the Athenian tribute, Ariadne fell in love with him and gave him a ball of thread — a clew — to navigate the Labyrinth and find his way out after killing the Minotaur. She is one of the most significant figures in the Minoan-derived myths of ancient Greece.

What happened to Ariadne after Theseus abandoned her?

Theseus left Ariadne asleep on the island of Naxos while sailing home. Most versions say the god Dionysus found her there and took her as his wife — elevating her from abandoned princess to goddess. Hesiod's Theogony names her as Dionysus's wife. Her crown (the Corona Borealis) was placed in the sky as a constellation. Some older variants, however, say Theseus abandoned her because Dionysus had already claimed her and appeared to Theseus in a dream demanding he leave.

Why did Theseus abandon Ariadne?

Ancient sources give conflicting reasons. The simplest: he forgot her or simply abandoned her. A darker reading in some sources suggests she was pregnant and Theseus didn't want the complication. The more theological version says Dionysus appeared to him and commanded him to leave — that Ariadne was always destined for a god, not a mortal hero. Plutarch's Life of Theseus surveys several contradictory traditions without settling the question.

Was Ariadne a goddess?

Ariadne occupies an interesting religious position. Some ancient evidence suggests she was worshipped as a deity on Crete before the Theseus myth attached to her. On Cyprus, two separate cults existed — one honoring a mortal Ariadne who died in childbirth, another a divine Ariadne as wife of Dionysus. This double tradition suggests the mythological figure may overlay an older Minoan cult figure whose original identity was transformed by later Greek storytelling.
