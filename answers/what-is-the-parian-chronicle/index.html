What Is the Parian Chronicle?

The Parian Chronicle — known in Greek as the Marmor Parium — is a marble stele inscribed in 264-263 BCE on the island of Paros, in the Cyclades. It is one of the only surviving ancient Greek documents that attempts a continuous chronology, dating events from the legendary founding of Athens through to the year of inscription.

The chronicle dates events by counting backwards from its present — listing the number of years since mythological and historical events occurred. It records things like the date of the Trojan War, the birth of Homer, the first performance of tragedy in Athens, the Persian Wars, and major political events. It treats mythological events (the flood of Deucalion, the reign of Cecrops) with the same chronological seriousness as historical ones.

This is important for several reasons. First, it shows how a 3rd-century BCE Greek understood the relationship between myth and history — they were not separate categories. Second, it provides independent date estimates for events that we try to date through other means (Homer's composition, the Trojan War). Third, it was found on Paros — an island with marble quarries that supplied both the Parthenon and some of the most significant Cycladic sculpture.

Most of the stele is now in the Ashmolean Museum in Oxford. A fragment remains in Paros. The chronicle was found in pieces in the early 17th century, purchased by Thomas Howard, Earl of Arundel, and eventually donated to Oxford.

It is not a reliable history in the modern sense. Its mythological dates are not verifiable. But as a document of how the ancient Greeks understood their own past — what they thought happened, when they thought it happened, and what they thought mattered — it has no equal.
