What Is Kleos?

Kleos is the Greek word for glory — specifically, the kind of fame that survives death. It is why Achilles made the choice he did. It may be the most important concept in all of Homer.

What does kleos mean in Greek mythology?

Kleos (κλέος) is an ancient Greek word meaning 'glory' or 'fame' — specifically the undying reputation earned through great deeds that is sung about by poets. The word is related to the verb kluo, meaning 'to hear.' In Homeric epic, kleos is the highest prize a hero can win because it is the only form of immortality available to mortals: your body dies, but your name and deeds live forever in song. Homer's Iliad is itself an act of kleos-preservation.

Why was kleos important to Achilles?

In Book 9 of the Iliad, Achilles explicitly articulates his choice: he can live long and obscurely, or he can die young at Troy and win imperishable kleos. He is fully aware of both paths. His refusal to fight — and his eventual return — are both driven by kleos logic: his honor (timē) has been violated by Agamemnon, and fighting without recognition gives him no kleos. Only when his closest companion Patroclus dies does kleos yield to grief and vengeance.

What is the difference between kleos and timē?

Timē (τιμή) means honor or respect — specifically the recognition and material rewards a hero receives from his community for his deeds. Kleos is the lasting fame that results from those deeds. Timē is immediate and social; kleos is permanent and poetic. In the Iliad, Agamemnon taking Briseis from Achilles is a violation of timē. The undying reputation Achilles earns by returning to battle and killing Hector is kleos. Both are essential to Homeric heroism — they reinforce each other.

Does kleos appear in texts other than Homer?

Yes. Pindar's victory odes (Epinicia) from the 5th century BCE are essentially kleos-machines — they immortalize athletic victors through song in the same way Homer immortalized warriors. The concept appears in Hesiod, in the tragedians, and in Thucydides's account of Pericles's Funeral Oration, where he argues that Athens itself has won imperishable kleos. The word appears in Linear B tablets as a personal name, suggesting it predates Homer and was already culturally embedded in Mycenaean Greece.
