Was the Trojan War Real?

There are nine cities buried at Hisarlik, Turkey. Heinrich Schliemann, who excavated the site in 1871-1873, dug straight through the layer that matches Homer's timeline. He was looking for gold and found it — in Troy II, which dates to roughly 2400 BCE. Homer's Troy would be Troy VIh or VIIa, which dates to roughly 1300-1180 BCE. Schliemann destroyed much of what he was looking for in order to reach something older.

The Hittite tablets provide the strongest non-Greek evidence. The Wilusa Treaty (the Alaksandu Treaty), dated to approximately 1280 BCE, records a Hittite agreement with the king of a city called Wilusa. The location of Wilusa matches Hisarlik. The name Wilusa corresponds, through sound change, to Ilios — Homer's other name for Troy. This is a contemporary document, written during the period when the Trojan War is supposed to have occurred, naming a city in the right location with the right name.

Manfred Korfmann's excavations from 1988 to 2005 established that Troy VIIa was a substantial city with extensive lower settlements — much larger than previously thought, matching Homer's description of a city worth fighting over for ten years.

The honest answer: there almost certainly was a city at Hisarlik that Greeks fought over in the late Bronze Age. Whether it happened as Homer describes — with the specific kings, gods, individual heroes, and decade-long siege — is a different question. Homer wrote four hundred years after the events he describes, drawing on oral tradition. The Iliad is not a history. It is a poem about what those events meant. The city was real. The war probably had a basis in real conflict. The poem is Homer's.
