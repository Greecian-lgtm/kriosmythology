Who Killed the Minotaur?

Everyone knows Theseus killed the Minotaur. Fewer people know that ancient sources disagree on whether he used a sword, his bare hands — or whether the Minotaur was human.

Who killed the Minotaur?

Theseus, son of King Aegeus of Athens (or the god Poseidon), killed the Minotaur in the Labyrinth beneath the palace of King Minos on Crete. He was aided by Ariadne, Minos's daughter, who gave him a thread to navigate the maze. The myth is told in detail by Plutarch in his Life of Theseus and referenced across many ancient sources including Diodorus Siculus and Ovid's Metamorphoses.

How did Theseus kill the Minotaur?

Ancient sources differ. Most say Theseus used his fists or a sword. The mythographer Apollodorus says he 'killed the Minotaur by striking it with his fists.' Ovid's Metamorphoses implies the Minotaur was killed with a sword. Vase paintings — our oldest visual evidence — show the struggle in various ways: sometimes Theseus grabs the Minotaur by the horn, sometimes he drives a sword into it. No single version dominated the ancient tradition.

What was the Minotaur?

The Minotaur — Minotauros in Greek — means 'bull of Minos.' It was the offspring of Minos's wife Pasiphae and a bull sent by Poseidon, born with a human body and the head of a bull. Minos imprisoned it in the Labyrinth built by the craftsman Daedalus. Athens was required to send seven young men and seven young women to Crete every nine years as tribute, and these were fed to the Minotaur. The creature had no personal name in antiquity — it was simply called 'the bull of Minos' or Asterion.

Was the Minotaur based on a real creature?

No physical creature matched the description, but the myth likely reflects Minoan culture. Crete's Bronze Age Minoan civilization (c. 2700–1450 BCE) was famous for bull imagery — bull-leaping frescoes, bull-horned altars, and ritual bull sacrifice appear throughout Minoan archaeology. The Palace of Knossos, with its complex interconnected rooms, may have given rise to the Labyrinth legend. Some scholars suggest 'the Minotaur' symbolized a Minoan priest or king wearing a bull mask in ritual.
