How Many Men Actually Fought at Thermopylae?

Herodotus, in Histories Book 7, is the primary source for Thermopylae. He gives the Greek force at the pass as approximately 7,000 men. The 300 Spartans were the Spartan contingent — one component of a coalition force that included Thespians, Thebans, Phocians, Locrians, Arcadians, Corinthians, and others.

When Leonidas dismissed the Greek allies before the final stand, most withdrew. The 300 Spartans stayed. So did 700 Thespians — the entire fighting force of the city of Thespiae, fighting voluntarily. The Thespians had no king ordering them to die there. They stayed because Thespiae lay directly in the Persian line of march. If Thermopylae fell, Thespiae would be destroyed regardless. They died to the last man alongside the Spartans.

There were also approximately 400 Thebans who were compelled to remain, according to Herodotus — held as hostages to ensure Theban loyalty, not there by choice.

The 300 Spartans have been remembered because Sparta built the memory. They had the political infrastructure, the oral tradition, the monuments. The Thespians had their city burned by Xerxes immediately after the battle — Thespiae was destroyed precisely because of the stand its men made. There was no city left to build the memory.

Herodotus names Leonidas. He also names Dieneces, the Spartan who made the joke about fighting in the shade. What he records most carefully is numbers — and the numbers are 7,000 Greeks, not 300. The story of only 300 is a later simplification of a coalition action in which the Thespians arguably sacrificed more, and are remembered by almost nobody.
