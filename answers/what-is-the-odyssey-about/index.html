What Is Homer's Odyssey About?

The Odyssey is about a man trying to get home. It is also about whether home still exists when you get there — and whether you are still the person who left.

What is Homer's Odyssey about?

The Odyssey is an ancient Greek epic poem attributed to Homer, likely composed in the 8th century BCE. It follows Odysseus, king of Ithaca, on his ten-year journey home after the fall of Troy. Along the way he faces the Cyclops Polyphemus, the witch Circe, the Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, and a visit to the land of the dead. Meanwhile, his wife Penelope holds off over a hundred suitors who assume Odysseus is dead, and his son Telemachus searches for news of his father.

What is the main theme of the Odyssey?

Scholars identify several core themes: nostos (the Greek word for homecoming), identity and disguise, loyalty and betrayal, cunning versus brute strength, and the relationship between gods and mortals. The poem's central tension is whether Odysseus can return and reclaim his home, his marriage, and his identity after twenty years of absence. Emily Wilson's 2017 translation brought new attention to the poem's treatment of slavery and the power structures that Odysseus's household rests on.

Is the Odyssey a true story?

The Odyssey is not a historical account, but it reflects real geography and real Bronze Age institutions. Scholars debate which mythological locations correspond to real places — possible candidates for the Cyclops's island include Sicily, and the Sirens' rocks have been placed in the Strait of Messina. The Mycenaean civilization Homer describes — palace economies, bronze armor, heroic gift exchange — is historically attested in Linear B tablets and archaeology. The poem is best understood as legend shaped around historical memory.

How long is Homer's Odyssey?

The Odyssey contains 24 books and approximately 12,110 lines of dactylic hexameter verse. It is generally considered shorter and more unified in structure than Homer's Iliad (15,693 lines). A complete reading aloud takes roughly 15–18 hours. The 24-book structure may reflect Alexandrian editorial work rather than Homer's original organization.
