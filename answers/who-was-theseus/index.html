Who Was Theseus?

He killed the Minotaur, sailed with Heracles, and supposedly invented democracy. Theseus is Athens's founding myth — but the ancient sources are far stranger than the clean hero story.

Who was Theseus in Greek mythology?

Theseus was the mythological king of Athens and the son of either the god Poseidon or the mortal king Aegeus — ancient sources give both. He is credited with killing the Minotaur in the Labyrinth of Knossos, unifying the villages of Attica into Athens, and establishing many of the city's founding institutions. Plutarch's Life of Theseus, written around 100 CE, is the most detailed ancient biography we have.

Did Theseus really kill the Minotaur?

The myth says yes: Theseus sailed to Crete as one of seven youths and seven maidens offered as tribute to King Minos, navigated the Labyrinth with a thread given by Minos's daughter Ariadne, killed the Minotaur, and escaped. Whether the story reflects a historical memory of Mycenaean-Minoan relations — possibly Cretan tribute or bull-leaping rituals — is actively debated by scholars.

What happened to Theseus after the Minotaur?

After killing the Minotaur, Theseus abandoned Ariadne on the island of Naxos and sailed home. He forgot to change his ship's sails from black to white — the agreed signal of success — and his father Aegeus, watching from the cliffs, assumed Theseus was dead and threw himself into the sea. That sea is named the Aegean in his memory. Theseus later became king, attempted to kidnap Persephone from the underworld, and died in exile on Skyros when king Lycomedes pushed him off a cliff.

Why was Theseus important to Athens?

Theseus was credited with synoikismos — the political unification of Attica's scattered villages into a single Athenian state. This made him Athens's founding hero in the same way Romulus was Rome's. The Athenians recovered what they claimed were his bones from Skyros in 476 BCE and built a temple in his honor, the Theseum. He represented the democratic ideal of a king who ruled by consent rather than force.
